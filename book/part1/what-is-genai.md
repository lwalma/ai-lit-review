# What is Generative AI?

## Generative AI Explained

Before you learn how you can use GenAI tools to assist you in the literature research and writing process, it is important to have a basic understanding of how GenAI works. GenAI, short for generative artificial intelligence, refers to computer systems that perform tasks that typically require human intelligence and generate new, human-like content such as text, images or videos.

An important principle of GenAI is that it doesn’t understand what it is doing. Rather, it generates information based on input from a user and a set of rules. This concept is often explained through John Searle’s Chinese Room Experiment. Watch <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8NUd1FX9dYs" target="_blank">this video</a> to learn about this thought experiment.

In this analogy, the question written by the person outside the room is your prompt, the room is the GenAI tool, the book with instructions are the rules that the tool uses, and the response from the person inside the room is the GenAI output.

About the rules: a lot of the GenAI tools that are currently thriving make use of Large Language Models (LLMs) that work based on word prediction. Watch the video below to learn more about how LLMs use word prediction to generate output.

Different GenAI tools make use of different LLMs. For example, ChatGPT's LLM is called GPT. Microsoft's Copilot also uses GPT, but there are other LLMs as well, such as Google's Gemini or Anthropic's Claude. Since 2022, a lot of different versions of these LLMs have been launched, each version operating faster and more effective than the previous one. You can find a timeline of LLM releases on <a href="https://llm-release-dashboard.vercel.app/" target="_blank">this website</a>.

Tools like Copilot, ChatGPT, Claude and Gemini can be seen as interfaces that use one of these LLMs to perform tasks. There are differences between these tools in what the interface looks like, what LLM the tool uses and what sources it draws from, as well as what you can use it for. Since there are a lot of different GenAI tools with different purposes, it is important to select a GenAI tool that aligns with your goals. In part 2 of this book, you will learn more about the differences between general and academic tools and how you can select the most suitable GenAI tool for specific steps in the research and writing process.

## Limitations and Considerations

GenAI tools have a great potential, especially because they can perform tasks way faster than humans can. Even though the models that are used by GenAI tools are getting more accurate over time, there are some important limitations that you should be aware of before using GenAI, especially for research purposes.

````{tab-set}

```{tab-item} Inaccurate output

As mentioned in the video about the Chinese Room experiment, GenAI tools do not know what they are doing. They simply follow the rules that they were trained with, but they are unaware of the content they work with. Because of that, a GenAI tool does not necessarily generate content that is true: GenAI output often contains inaccuracies ('hallucinations'). Some scholars would even go as far as saying that truth in GenAI output is a coincidence: it sometimes happens to generate truthful content. There is an abundance of examples in which people used GenAI tools to generate text with inaccuracies, but did not notice. In 2023, two New York lawyers submitted a legal brief that included multiple <a href="https://apnews.com/article/artificial-intelligence-chatgpt-courts-e15023d7e6fdf4f099aa122437dbb59b" target="_blank">fake case citations</a> that were generated by ChatGPT, and were <a href="https://www.reuters.com/legal/new-york-lawyers-sanctioned-using-fake-chatgpt-cases-legal-brief-2023-06-22/" target="_blank">punished</a> with a $5.000 fine. More recently, in January 2026, a Dutch couple's <a href="https://www.reuters.com/lifestyle/dutch-couples-marriage-annulled-due-chatgpt-speech-2026-01-09/" target="_blank">marriage was annulled</a> after the person officiating the wedding used a ChatGPT-generated speech that failed to meet legal requirements.

There are also examples of problems with inaccurate AI output in academia. In January 2026, the rector of Ghent University used several <a href="https://www.vrt.be/vrtnws/en/2026/01/08/ghent-university-rector-petra-de-sutter-uses-fabricated-quotes-i/" target="_blank">fabricated quotes</a> in her inaugural speech, and, as a result of the commotion afterwards, <a href="https://delta.tudelft.nl/en/article/rector-of-ghent-university-withdraws-from-honorary-doctorate-at-university-of-amsterdam-after-ai-speech" target="_blank">withdraw from an honorary doctorate</a> she was set to receive from the University of Amsterdam. In July 2026, research from the Dutch magazine <a href="https://www.groene.nl/artikel/de-wetenschap-wordt-in-sneltempo-vervuild" target="_blank">De Groene Amsterdammer</a> revealed that, since the release of ChatGPT, the proportion of articles containing non-existing references has increased sevenfold (read about the research in English <a href="https://delta.tudelft.nl/en/article/number-of-ghost-references-in-scientific-articles-growing-rapidly" target="_blank">here</a>). International research performed by <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00969-z" target="_blank">Nature</a> shows a similar pattern: ten of thousands of scientific publications from 2025 include non-existing references, probably generated by AI.

To avoid including inaccuracies in your text, you should never take GenAI output for granted. Instead, always critically check the output and compare it with ‘traditional’ sources like scientific journal papers. That means that working with GenAI tools requires knowledge about the topic you are working on. If you do not have enough knowledge about the topic, you will not be able to critically check the GenAI output. Instead, you should first acquire enough knowledge through 'traditional' sources before working with GenAI tools. If checking the GenAI output against "traditional" sources takes too much time (for example, because you do not know enough about the topic yet), it might be advisable to not use GenAI and stick to "traditional" sources.

Additionally, you should be very careful with sources that are provided by GenAI tools. General AI tools, especially when you are using tools that are not connected to internet, often provide sources that look reliable, but turn out to be fake, as some of the examples above also highlight. Also polishing your reference list with general AI tools is risky: the AI often changes the source description resulting in a non-existing sources in your reference list. Alternatively, use academic AI tools to look for sources and compile your reference list yourself. You will learn more about using academic AI tools in part 2 of this book.

```

```{tab-item} Biases

GenAI tools are trained with real-life data and therefore can reflect and amplify biases from human thinking. For example, <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-55686-2" target="_blank">research</a> has shown that GenAI tools are often biased against women and people of colour. This is not only the case for text generation, but biases are also prominent in <a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2023-generative-ai-bias/" target="_blank">AI-generated images</a>. Other biases, for example confirmation bias (the tendency to search for and favour information that confirms your beliefs), can also affect the data that is used for training GenAI tools, and therefore also affect the output. See <a href="https://www.chapman.edu/ai/bias-in-ai.aspx" target="_blank">this overview from Chapman University</a> for more examples of biases that occur in GenAI tools and how that affects the output.

Because of the risk of biases, it is important to always critically check the GenAI output. For example, beware of gender, racial or other stereotypes in AI-generated content. Additionally, check claims that are made in GenAI output against "traditional" sources and your own knowledge, to check if these claims are well justified with proper evidence.

```

```{tab-item} Cognitive offloading and loss of creativity

Completely outsourcing tasks to GenAI tools is not smart, not only because of the possible inaccuracies and biases in the output, but also because it leads to overreliance on GenAI. Overreliance, in turn, can lead to loss of creativity and critical thinking skills ("cognitive offloading"), as well as a potential decrease in ability to detect misinformation, as proven by a study from the <a href="https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3772318.3790656" target="_blank">Massachusetts Institute of Technology</a>. In addition, another <a href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.08872v1" target="_blank">MIT study</a> shows that students who had to write an essay with the use of GenAI tools showed less brain connectivity than students who did not use AI, proving the effect of cognitive offloading as a result of AI dependence.

To avoid cognitive offloading and loss of creativity, it is essential to only use GenAI tools as an assistant, rather than outsourcing your tasks to an AI. Because of this, it is important that you master research and writing skills yourself. Once you do, you can use GenAI tools to assist you, while always critically assessing the output. Use your own critical thinking skills to evaluate the quality of GenAI output. Use your knowledge about the topic and academic practices to avoid inaccuracies and comply to academic standards. And use your own creativity to come up with interesting research topics and a well written text in your own voice, rather than basic, unoriginal GenAI output.

```

```{tab-item} Privacy and copyright

GenAI tools are trained with data that is publicly available. However, the authors of the texts that are used for training purposes did not give permission for this. Although GenAI tools do not typically copy exact passages, they do extract patterns, styles and ideas. This leads to a grey area of potential copyright infringement. In addition, it is often unclear how GenAI tools use the input (prompts) from users. These could also be (and often are) used for training purposes.

It is therefore important to be careful with your GenAI input. Some guidelines include: (1) never insert personal details, (2) never insert confidential information and (3) only insert work from others if you have their explicit consent (also to avoid copyright infringement). Be aware that your input may be used for training purposes. If you don't want your research project to be used for these purposes (for example, because you are dealing with sensitive information or because you don't want others to know what you are working on), it is advised not to use GenAI tools.

```

```{tab-item} Environmental impact

The <a href="https://news.mit.edu/2025/explained-generative-ai-environmental-impact-0117" target="_blank">environmental impact of GenAI</a> is significant: it uses great amounts of water and energy for running the models (i.e., responding to prompts) and training them. In addition, building data centers and servers that run these models requires great amounts of (raw) materials. Specifically, a <a href="https://www.unep.org/news-and-stories/story/ai-has-environmental-problem-heres-what-world-can-do-about" target="_blank">United Nations Environment Programme report</a> shows that (1) making a 2 kg computer requires 800 kg of raw materials, (2) the microchips that power AI need rare earth elements, (3) data centers produce electronic waste, which often contains hazardous substances such as mercury and lead, (4) global AI-related infrastructure consumes six times more water than Denmark, a country of 6 million and (5) one request made through ChatGPT consumes 10 times the electricity of a Google search. For more information about the environmental impact, consult this <a href="https://www.greenpeace.org/international/story/82486/ai-energy-environment-democracy/" target="_blank">Greenpeace report</a>, which also highlights the dependency on Big Tech.

Because of the significance of the environmental impact, it is advisable to take this into account when considering using a GenAI tool. For simple tasks, it might be better to do it without GenAI to limit the environmental impact.
````
